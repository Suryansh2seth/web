On the aesthetics of unfinished things

Section 01 // Draft

There is a specific kind of honesty found in blueprints that rarely survives the transition to a finished building. When a project is still on the drafting table, its structural logic is laid bare. You can see the load-bearing walls, the tension cables, the exact coordinates where the foundation meets the earth.

Once the rendering is applied, all of that geometry is hidden behind drywall and paint. The final product looks clean, but it lies about how it stands up.

"A wireframe is a promise. A finished UI is a contract. I prefer the promise."

The Drafting Table as a Medium

I have been thinking a lot lately about why I prefer the coming-soon phase of a website to the actual launch. It is not just procrastination, though that is certainly a factor. It is the fact that a site in progress is a space of pure potential. Every empty grid cell is a question waiting to be answered.

When you build in the open, you are essentially inviting the viewer to look at your scaffolding. You are saying: Here is how I think. Here is how I structure my thoughts. The final output is just a byproduct of this process.

Notes on Tolerance

In engineering, tolerance is the permissible limit of variation in a physical dimension. In writing, tolerance is the amount of ambiguity you are willing to leave on the page. I am trying to build a site with high tolerance. A site that admits it is not finished. A site that leaves room for the reader to project their own meaning onto the empty spaces.

Area reserved on the page. Foundation poured, nothing built yet. And perhaps that is exactly how it should stay.
